Viability

We tested three types viability.

Every field in the instrument exists to feed a named indicator. So, the key viability question is for each indicator, were counties able to produce a usable number? 

one · Pragmatic

Did counties respond at all?

Reach, item-level completion, identifiable contact. The lowest bar — whether the instrument gets into the field and comes back. The pilot cleared this for four of six invited counties.

two · Usability

Could we use what they sent?

Identity-check pass rate. Three of four responses came close to reporting end of period staff number is equal to start of period staff minus departures plus new hires (with some wiggle room). Voluntary plus involuntary equalled total turnover in all four. One reported headcount greater than Full-Time Equivalent Authorized, which could indicate an overhire pattern the instrument does not currently flag.

three · Comparability

Does the same indicator mean the same thing across counties?

Definitional consistency and the ability to aggregate data across counties. This would be required to publish a statewide indicator. Eight of fourteen indicators meet this requirement, but six don't.

The themes · Why six don't compute

The pattern is a data-systems pattern, not an instrument pattern.

All fourteen indicators are calculated from required input fields in the survey. Cutting items would mean cutting indicators. Some indicators ask counties to produce aggregates their Human Resources Information System may not currently return as a single query. The additional work required may be a hurdle to reporting. 

Tenure indicators don't compute because counties cannot pull aggregate days-employed at a period boundary.

Two of four counties skipped the Tenure block entirely (Monterey first half of 2025; Sacramento second half of 2025). They self-disclosed that running a sum of days-employed across all current and former staff at a fixed boundary is not a query their Human Resources Information System produces. Mendocino and San Diego (second half of 2025) returned the sums by hand-counting from personnel records.

Education at hire appears unavailable. No pilot county returned it.

The indicator requires counties have data for each new hire's degree at the time of hire, partitioned by six degree categories. This may not be a queryable Human Resources Information System aggregate in any of the systems the pilot reached. The two counties that populated other Education blocks did so by hand-counting personnel files.

Current-education breakdowns differ by what counties can query.

Sacramento (second half of 2025) returned a single aggregate degree-counted total without breaking down by degree type — the Human Resources Information System query they ran returns "has a degree" but not "what kind." Mendocino returned the full degree-type partition. The difference is Human Resources Information System configuration, not effort.

Workforce Size, Turnover, and Career Development appear reportable for counties.

Eight of fourteen indicators were computable for each of the four complete responses. Headcount, exits (voluntary and involuntary), promotions, lateral moves, demotions, and training hours come from systems counties already query routinely. These are the indicators appear possible to scale.

Options for next steps

Options, given available evidence. 

Not mutually exclusive.

A
Option A — Build Human Resources Information System query templates counties can run in their HR systems.

Two Structured Query Language templates — one for the Tenure block (days-employed sums) and one for the Education at hire block (per-hire degree partitions). Counties run them against their Human Resources Information System and paste the aggregates into the survey. Reduces respondent burden without dropping any indicator.

B
Option B — Tag every item by source system.

Human Resources Information System · Learning Management System · Manual · Human Resources Information System query template. Counties stop guessing where to find each number. Analysts know what to weight in cross-county comparisons.

C
Option C — Add an explicit overhire flag and an in-survey reconciliation prompt.

A warning that says "we are running above Full-Time Equivalent Authorized". A reconciliation prompt at the end of each block, such as "these numbers don't add up; review before submitting". This would help catch inconsistencies before submission.

D
Option D — Pilot further with attention to geographic and county-size diversity.

Deliberate diversification of the next pilot cohort by geography, county size, and Human Resources Information System maturity. May reveal whether the pilot's findings hold under broader sampling.

E
Option E — Further calibrate the indicators with participating pilot counties. 

Interview Sacramento (second half of 2025), Mendocino (second half of 2025), and San Diego (second half of 2025) to calibrate what data collection signals against their reporting experience. 

F
Option F — Staged rollout: revise · re-pilot · verify · scale.

Revise, then re-pilot at ten to twelve counties selected for geographic and county-size diversity. Then reassess and consider scaling up with other statewide workforce data collection efforts.
